_Goal: learn the handful of candlestick patterns that actually carry information, understand the trapped-trader psychology behind each one, and internalise the rule that a pattern is only a signal at a level, with volume, and after confirmation._

## Why This Week Matters

In Week 2 you learned to read one candle — its Open, High, Low, Close, its body and its wicks. This week you learn what happens when candles line up into recognisable shapes. That matters because intraday trading is not about predicting the future; it is about reading, in real time, which side (buyers or sellers) just lost control and got trapped. A candlestick pattern is a fossil of that battle. When you can read it, you stop guessing direction and start reacting to evidence.

But there is a trap in the patterns themselves. Beginners memorise 40 shapes from a textbook and then see them everywhere — because random price movement produces "hammers" and "dojis" all day long. The skill is not naming shapes. The skill is knowing **where** a shape is allowed to mean something, **who** it traps, and **whether** the next candle confirms the story. A hammer floating in the middle of a range is noise. The same hammer sitting on a Bank Nifty support level you drew, printed on a volume spike, and followed by a strong green candle — that is a tradeable event. This week is where reading candles becomes reading intent.

Everything here is still paper only. You will not risk a single rupee until Week 12. This week you are training your eyes and your patience.

## Concept 1 — A Pattern Is a Story About Who Got Trapped

Before any specific shape, understand the mechanism that makes candlestick patterns work at all.

Price on the NSE moves because orders hit the order book. The **order book** is the live list of buy orders (bids) and sell orders (asks) waiting at each price. When aggressive buyers eat through all the sell orders at a price, price rises; when aggressive sellers hit all the bids, price falls. A candle is simply a five-minute photograph of that fight.

A **reversal pattern** forms when one side commits hard in a direction, runs out of fresh orders, and then gets overwhelmed by the other side. The traders who committed late are now **trapped** — they are holding a position that is immediately underwater, and to get out they must trade against themselves (a trapped buyer must sell to exit, adding fuel to the fall). This is the engine behind every reversal candle. You are not looking at a shape; you are looking at a group of traders about to be forced to bail out.

```
Anatomy recap — the two things every pattern is built from
    |            upper wick  = price pushed up here, got rejected
  ┌───┐          body        = open-to-close, shows who WON the slice
  │   │
  └───┘
    |            lower wick  = price pushed down here, got rejected
   Body = conviction.  Wick = rejection.  Close = who held the rope.
```

Keep three filters in your head for the entire week. A pattern is only a signal when all three are true:

1. **Location** — it sits at a support or resistance level you drew in advance (Week 4 goes deeper on levels, but you already know the idea from Week 2).
2. **Volume** — the pattern candle prints on higher-than-recent volume, proving real participants, not a thin drift.
3. **Confirmation** — the _next_ candle continues the story (more on this in Concept 6).

Miss any one and you have a shape, not a signal.

## Concept 2 — The Doji Family: Indecision and Exhaustion

A **doji** is a candle where the Open and Close are almost identical, leaving a tiny or nonexistent body. Neither side won the slice. By itself a doji says "balance" — but _where_ it appears changes everything, because a doji after a long one-directional run is the first visible sign that the winning side is running out of ammunition.

### Standard doji

```
Standard doji — pure indecision
      |
      |
   ───┼───     open ≈ close (tiny body)
      |
      |
Long wicks both sides: buyers and sellers fought, nobody closed ahead.
```

Psychology: whoever was in control (say buyers in an uptrend) pushed as usual, but this time an equal wall of sellers absorbed them. Nobody is trapped yet, but the momentum has stalled. Treat a standard doji as a "pay attention" flag, not an entry.

### Dragonfly doji (bullish bias at support)

```
Dragonfly doji — sellers rejected, close snaps back to open
   ───┼───     open = high = close (all near the top)
      |
      |
      |        long lower wick
Sellers drove price down all slice; buyers reclaimed every tick by the close.
```

The dragonfly is effectively a doji-shaped hammer. Sellers who sold the lows are now trapped — price closed right back at the top, so their fresh shorts are already losing. At support, this is a bullish tell.

### Gravestone doji (bearish bias at resistance)

```
Gravestone doji — buyers rejected, close falls back to open
      |
      |        long upper wick
      |
   ───┼───     open = low = close (all near the bottom)
Buyers drove price up all slice; sellers reclaimed every tick by the close.
```

The mirror image. Buyers who chased the highs are trapped; the close came back down to where the candle opened. At resistance, this is a bearish tell.

**Failure case:** dojis are common and cheap. In a quiet mid-morning range you will see a dozen. A doji with no level and no volume behind it is just the market breathing. Never trade a doji in isolation — it tells you to wait, not to act.

## Concept 3 — Single-Candle Rejections: Hammer, Hanging Man, Shooting Star, Inverted Hammer

These four are the same two shapes, but their meaning flips depending on the trend that came before. This is the single most common thing beginners get wrong, so slow down here.

### Hammer (bullish reversal, appears after a decline, at support)

A small body near the **top** of the range with a long lower wick at least twice the body, and little or no upper wick.

```
Hammer — buyers reject lower prices
   ┌──┐     small body near the top
   └──┘
    |
    |        lower wick ≥ 2× body
    |
Sellers pushed down hard; buyers absorbed the selling and closed near the top.
```

Mechanism: during the candle, sellers pressed price down and short-sellers piled in near the lows. Then buyers stepped in with size, ate all the sell orders, and dragged the close back up. The late sellers are trapped below a rising price. When this happens _at support after a downtrend_, it is a classic bullish signal.

### Hanging man (bearish warning — identical shape, wrong place)

The exact same shape as a hammer, but it appears **after an uptrend, at resistance**. Now the long lower wick means something ominous: for the first time in the rally, sellers were strong enough to drive price sharply down mid-candle, even if buyers rescued the close. It reveals that supply has arrived. It needs strong confirmation (a red candle next) because the close itself was bullish.

### Shooting star (bearish reversal, appears after a rally, at resistance)

Small body near the **bottom** of the range, long upper wick at least twice the body, little or no lower wick.

```
Shooting star — sellers reject higher prices
    |
    |        upper wick ≥ 2× body
    |
   ┌──┐     small body near the bottom
   └──┘
Buyers chased price up; sellers slammed it back and closed near the low.
```

Mechanism: buyers chased the breakout, exhausted their orders at the highs, and sellers dumped into them. The chasers are now trapped long at the top. At resistance after an uptrend, this is a strong bearish signal.

### Inverted hammer (bullish — same shape as shooting star, wrong place)

The shooting-star shape appearing **after a downtrend, at support**. The long upper wick shows buyers _tried_ to lift price for the first time in the decline. It hints the tide is turning but it is weaker than a hammer, because the close is still near the lows — you badly need a green confirmation candle before acting.

The lesson: **shape + preceding trend + location = meaning.** The same silhouette is bullish in one place and bearish in another.

## Concept 4 — Two-Candle Takeovers: Engulfing, Piercing, and Dark Cloud

Single candles are fast but noisy. Two-candle patterns are more reliable because they show a _handover_ of control from one candle to the next.

### Bullish engulfing (at support)

A small red candle, then a big green candle whose body completely swallows the previous red body (green open below the red close, green close above the red open).

```
Bullish engulfing — buyers overwhelm the prior sellers
          ┌────┐
   ┌──┐   │    │   green body fully engulfs
   │▓▓│   │    │   the small red body
   └──┘   │    │
          └────┘
   (red)  (green)
Day-1 sellers who sold the red candle are now underwater.
```

Mechanism: the red candle made sellers confident. The next candle opens lower (sellers press again) but buyers arrive in force and drive the close above the _entire_ previous candle. Every seller from the red candle and the early part of the green candle is trapped. The bigger the engulfing body and the higher the volume, the more people are trapped and the stronger the signal.

### Bearish engulfing (at resistance)

The mirror: a small green candle swallowed by a big red one. The buyers from the green candle are trapped long as price collapses through their entries.

### Piercing pattern (bullish, softer than engulfing)

Two candles at support: a big red candle, then a green candle that opens _below_ the red low (a gap down) but closes back **above the midpoint** of the red candle's body — without fully engulfing it.

```
Piercing — buyers reclaim more than half the red candle
   ┌──┐
   │▓▓│  · · · · ·  midpoint of red body
   │▓▓│   ┌───┐
   └──┘   │   │  green closes above the red midpoint
          └───┘   (opened below red low, then rallied)
```

It is a partial takeover — the buyers did not win outright, but they clawed back most of the ground, trapping the traders who shorted the gap-down open.

### Dark cloud cover (bearish mirror of piercing)

At resistance: a big green candle, then a red candle that opens _above_ the green high but closes back below the midpoint of the green body. The gap-up buyers are trapped.

The rule of thumb: **engulfing &gt; piercing/dark cloud** in strength, because engulfing is a total reversal of the prior candle while piercing/dark cloud is only a partial one.

## Concept 5 — Three-Candle Reversals and the Marubozu

### Morning star (bullish, at support)

Three candles: a big red candle (sellers in control), then a small-bodied candle or doji (often gapping down — indecision, sellers exhausting), then a big green candle that closes well into the first candle's body.

```
Morning star — dawn after the sell-off (read left → right)
   ┌──┐
   │▓▓│                      ┌────┐
   │▓▓│                      │    │  strong green,
   │▓▓│                      │    │  closes into
   └──┘         ──           │    │  candle 1's body
              (small/doji)   └────┘
   day 1        day 2         day 3
   sellers      exhaustion    buyers seize control
```

Mechanism across three slices: sellers dominate (candle 1), then momentum dies and neither side pushes (candle 2 — this is where trapped sellers stop getting paid), then buyers take over decisively (candle 3). The small middle candle is the pivot: it is the moment the selling ran dry. Because you watched control shift over 15 minutes instead of 5, false signals are filtered out — which is why three-candle patterns are slower but more trustworthy.

### Evening star (bearish, at resistance)

The mirror image: big green → small indecision candle → big red closing into the green body. Buyers exhaust, then sellers seize control. Dusk after the rally.

### Marubozu (continuation / conviction)

A candle with a **full body and virtually no wicks** — open equals the high (or low) and close equals the other extreme.

```
Green marubozu        Red marubozu
   ┌──┐  close=high      ┌──┐  open=high
   │  │                  │▓▓│
   │  │                  │▓▓│
   └──┘  open=low        └──┘  close=low
No wicks: one side controlled every tick, open to close.
```

A green marubozu means buyers controlled the entire slice with zero pullback — pure momentum. A red marubozu is the same for sellers. Marubozus are not reversal signals; they are **conviction signals**. They are most useful for _confirming_ a breakout: a marubozu that closes above a resistance level tells you the break has force behind it, not a half-hearted poke.

## Concept 6 — The Confirmation Candle: The Discipline That Saves You

Here is the single most valuable habit in this week. A reversal pattern is a _hypothesis_, not a fact. The **confirmation candle** is the next candle after the pattern, and it must continue the story before you commit.

- After a hammer or bullish engulfing at support, confirmation is the **next candle trading above the pattern's high** (buyers followed through).
- After a shooting star or bearish engulfing at resistance, confirmation is the **next candle trading below the pattern's low** (sellers followed through).

Why this matters mechanically: a hammer proves buyers showed up _once_. It does not prove they will keep buying. Roughly half the pretty reversal candles you will ever see simply fail — price drifts back and the "reversal" evaporates. Waiting one candle for confirmation costs you a few points of entry but filters out a large share of losers. You trade slightly later, but you trade far fewer traps. For a beginner on paper, confirmation is not optional. Build the reflex now: pattern → wait one candle → confirm → only then plan the trade.

## Worked Examples

Both examples are hypothetical, with illustrative numbers, marked clearly. They are teaching devices, not predictions.

### Example 1 — Hammer at Bank Nifty support, with confirmation

It is 10:45 AM IST. Last week Bank Nifty bounced twice at **48,000**, so you drew a support zone there before the open. Today price falls toward it on the 5-minute chart.

- **10:45 candle:** Open 48,040, High 48,060, Low **47,955**, Close 48,045. Body is tiny (5 points, near the top), lower wick is 90 points — a clean **hammer** sitting right on your 48,000 zone.
- **Volume:** clearly higher than the previous several candles. Real buyers absorbed the sellers. Filter 1 (location) ✓, Filter 2 (volume) ✓.

Here is the before/after of what a hammer bouncing at support looks like:

```
Bank Nifty 5-min — hammer rejects the 48,000 support, then bounces

  price
   48,300 ┤                                   ╭─── target zone
   48,200 ┤        ╲                        ╭─╯
   48,100 ┤          ╲                    ╭─╯   confirmation
   48,045 ┤ ···········╲···············┌──┐·· candle trades
   48,000 ┼─ SUPPORT ───╲───────────── │▓▓│ ─── above hammer high
   47,955 ┤              └┐ hammer low └──┘
          └───────────────┴──────────────────────────► time
                      10:45  wick pierces 48,000, closes back above
```

You do **not** enter yet. You wait for the confirmation candle (Filter 3).

- **10:50 candle (confirmation):** trades up through the hammer's high of 48,060 and closes at 48,110. Buyers followed through. Now you have a setup.
- **Entry:** 48,065, just above the hammer high once confirmed.
- **Stop-loss:** 47,945, just below the hammer's wick low. Risk = 120 points.
- **Target:** next resistance at 48,305. Reward = 240 points.
- **Reward:risk = 2:1** ✓ — meets the ≥ 1:2 rule.

If instead the 10:50 candle had closed _below_ 47,945, the hammer failed, no confirmation, no trade — you lost nothing. And if price had later closed below 47,945 after entry, your stop takes you out for a small, pre-defined loss. Every trade has a stop; you risk only 1–2% of paper capital; you never move the stop away to "give it room."

**Counter-case:** the identical hammer shape printing at 48,127 — nowhere near a level, on thin volume — is a non-event. Same silhouette, no context, no trade. Rejecting that trade is as much a win as taking the good one.

### Example 2 — Bearish engulfing at Reliance resistance, and a failed pattern

It is 1:30 PM IST. Reliance has rallied all morning and stalls at **2,980**, a level where it topped out yesterday. You marked it pre-market.

- **1:30 candle:** small green, Open 2,972, Close 2,978. Buyers still nudging up, but the body is weak right under resistance.

- **1:35 candle:** Open 2,979, then sellers hammer it down to Close **2,965** — a big red body that fully engulfs the prior green body. **Bearish engulfing** at resistance, on volume well above the morning average. Filters 1 and 2 ✓. The buyers who bought the 1:30 green candle near 2,978 are now trapped long.

- **1:40 candle (confirmation):** trades below the engulfing candle's low of 2,963 and closes 2,955. Sellers followed through ✓.

- **Entry (short):** 2,962, just below the engulfing low, on confirmation.

- **Stop-loss:** 2,983, just above the resistance and the pattern high. Risk = 21 points.

- **Target:** intraday support / VWAP area near 2,920. Reward = 42 points.

- **Reward:risk = 2:1** ✓.

Now the instructive failure. Suppose the 1:40 confirmation candle had instead opened weak but _closed back at 2,979_, negating the sellers. No confirmation → no trade. Beginners who skip confirmation would already be short at 2,962 and watching price climb back into their stop. This is exactly the loss that the one-candle wait removes. The pattern was real; the follow-through was not; and only follow-through pays.

## Common Mistakes (and the fix)

**1. Trading patterns in the middle of nowhere**._Why beginners do it:_ the shape looks textbook-perfect, and naming it feels like skill. _The fix:_ demand location. If the candle is not at a support/resistance level you drew _before_ it formed, it does not exist as a signal. A perfect hammer in dead air is a coincidence, not a setup.

**2. Ignoring the preceding trend**._Why:_ they memorise "hammer = buy" and "shooting-star shape = sell" as fixed rules. _The fix:_ meaning depends on context. A hammer shape after an uptrend is a hanging man (bearish warning); a shooting-star shape after a downtrend is an inverted hammer (bullish hint). Always ask: what came _before_ this candle?

**3. Entering on the pattern candle instead of waiting for confirmation**._Why:_ fear of missing the move; the pattern candle looks so convincing. _The fix:_ wait one candle. Enter only when the next candle continues the story (above the hammer high / below the shooting-star low). You sacrifice a few points and skip a large share of failed reversals.

**4. Forgetting volume**._Why:_ volume is an extra thing to look at, and price alone feels like enough. _The fix:_ turn the volume histogram on and keep it on. A reversal candle on below-average volume means few participants committed — weak conviction, easy to reverse. Real reversals show a volume spike because real money changed hands.

**5. Moving or removing the stop-loss**._Why:_ the trade goes against them and they hope it comes back; the stop feels "too tight." _The fix:_ the stop goes just beyond the pattern's wick and never moves against you. If you can't accept the loss at that stop, your position size is too big — fix the size, not the stop.

## Nuances & Edge Cases

- **Timeframe agreement.** A hammer on the 5-minute chart is stronger if the 15-minute chart also shows price at a level and stalling. Conflicting timeframes weaken any pattern. Use 15-min for bias, 5-min for the trigger.
- **The 9:15 open distorts everything.** The first one or two 5-minute candles absorb the overnight news gap and are unusually large and wicky. Patterns there are unreliable; many traders let the first 15 minutes settle before trusting candle signals.
- **Gaps change the pattern.** Morning stars and piercing patterns classically involve a gap on the middle/second candle. Intraday, real gaps mostly happen only at the 9:15 open (the market is continuous during the day), so the "gap" versions are more a daily-chart phenomenon. Do not force a gap definition onto continuous intraday candles.
- **Body-to-wick proportion is a spectrum, not a switch.** A "hammer" with a lower wick only 1.3× the body is a weak hammer. The 2× rule is a guideline; the cleaner the proportion, the more trustworthy the trap.
- **Round numbers and prior-day levels amplify patterns.** Bank Nifty at 48,000, Nifty at a round 100-point level, or a stock at yesterday's high — these are where institutions place orders, so rejections there are more meaningful than at a random price.
- **Auto square-off compresses late-day patterns.** MIS (intraday margin) positions are auto-squared-off by your broker around 3:20 PM IST. Reversal patterns forming at 3:10–3:20 have almost no time to play out, and the square-off itself can distort the last candles. Treat late-session signals with extra caution.
- **News overrides candles.** An RBI announcement or a stock-specific headline can blow through any pattern. Candlesticks read the crowd; they cannot read a press release you haven't seen.

## Key Takeaways

- A candlestick pattern is a fossil of a trapped trade — it only matters when it shows one side committing, running dry, and getting overwhelmed at a **level**, on **volume**.
- The same shape changes meaning with the preceding trend: hammer vs hanging man, inverted hammer vs shooting star. Always read context, not silhouette.
- Two-candle (engulfing, piercing, dark cloud) and three-candle (morning/evening star) patterns are slower but more reliable than single candles because they show control actually changing hands.
- The **confirmation candle** is non-negotiable: wait one candle for follow-through before planning any entry. It filters out a large share of failed reversals.
- Every setup gets a stop just beyond the signal candle's wick, risk 1–2% per trade, reward:risk ≥ 1:2 — and the stop never moves against you.
- Rejecting a pretty pattern that lacks a level, volume, or confirmation is a winning decision, not a missed one.

## This Week's Milestone

You can look at any live Nifty or Bank Nifty 5-minute chart, spot a reversal candle sitting on a level you drew in advance, state out loud who is trapped, confirm it with volume and a follow-through candle, and write a complete paper-trade plan (entry, stop, target, R:R ≥ 1:2) — while correctly and calmly rejecting the identical pattern when it appears with no level, no volume, or no confirmation.
