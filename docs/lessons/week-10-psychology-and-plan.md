# Week 10 — Psychology & Your Trading Plan

> **◀ Previous:** [Week 9 — Intensive Paper Trading](./week-09-intensive-paper-trading.md) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Course Home:** [Lessons Index](./README.md) &nbsp;|&nbsp; **Next ▶:** [Week 11 — Review & Refine](./week-11-review-and-refine.md)

*Goal: understand why your own brain is the biggest edge-killer in intraday trading, and build a written plan that makes your decisions for you before the market can make them for you.*

## Why This Week Matters

By now you can read a candle, mark a level, size a position, and place a stop-loss. On paper you have a method. Yet if you ask any experienced trader why beginners blow up their accounts, almost none will say "bad strategy." They will say "no discipline." That gap — between knowing the right thing and doing it under pressure — is where Week 10 lives.

Intraday trading is uniquely hostile to the human brain. You are making fast, real-money decisions in an environment of constant uncertainty, immediate feedback, and vivid loss. Evolution did not design us for this. The same mental shortcuts that kept our ancestors alive — fear loss, chase reward, remember the recent scary thing — actively sabotage a trader. So a professional does not try to "stay calm and think clearly" in the moment. That is a losing battle against biology. Instead, the professional builds a **system of rules and circuit-breakers** decided in advance, when the brain is calm, and then simply obeys them when the brain is not.

This week you will learn the mechanics of the four or five mental failures that account for most beginner losses, and you will write a one-page trading plan that removes those decisions from the heat of the session. This is arguably the most important week of the whole course. A mediocre strategy with iron discipline beats a brilliant strategy with none.

## Concept 1 — The Neuroscience-Lite of Why Trading Is Hard

You do not need a psychology degree, but you do need to know your opponent — and your opponent is your own wiring. Four mechanisms do most of the damage.

### Loss aversion — a loss hurts about twice as much as a gain feels good

Research in behavioural economics (Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory) found that people feel the pain of losing roughly twice as intensely as the pleasure of an equivalent gain. Losing ₹1,000 hurts about as much as winning ₹2,000 feels good.

Why does this matter mechanically? Because it makes you do the exact opposite of correct trading. When a trade goes against you, the pain of "booking" (accepting) the loss is so sharp that your brain begs you to avoid it — so you hold, hoping it comes back, which turns a small planned loss into a large unplanned one. Meanwhile, when a trade goes in your favour, you feel a jolt of relief and grab the small profit early to make the pleasant feeling permanent — cutting winners short. Loss aversion literally reverses the golden rule: *cut losses short, let winners run.* Left to instinct, you will let losses run and cut winners short.

### Dopamine — the reward chemical fires on anticipation, not outcome

Dopamine is a neurotransmitter tied to reward-seeking. Crucially, it spikes not when you *receive* a reward but when you *anticipate* one, especially when the reward is uncertain. A slot machine works on exactly this principle: the unpredictable maybe-payoff is more addictive than a guaranteed one.

The market is the biggest slot machine ever built. Every tick that goes your way delivers a small hit. This is why over-trading feels good even when it loses money — the *action* itself is the drug, not the profit. A flat, patient day with no trades feels unbearable to a dopamine-primed brain, so beginners invent reasons to click.

### Recency bias — the last few events feel like the whole truth

Your brain weights recent events far more heavily than older ones. Three losing trades in a row and you "know" your strategy is broken — even though your tested edge plays out over hundreds of trades, not three. Two quick wins and you feel invincible, so you double your size right before the market humbles you. Recency bias makes you abandon a good plan at exactly the wrong moment and inflate risk at exactly the wrong moment.

### Sunk-cost fallacy — throwing good money after bad

Once you have committed to something — a position, a view, a losing day — the brain hates to "waste" what it already put in. "I've already lost ₹3,000 today, I can't stop now, I have to make it back." The ₹3,000 is gone; it is a sunk cost and irrelevant to the next decision. But the fallacy keeps you trading to justify what you already spent, which is precisely how a bad day becomes a catastrophic one.

```
How the four biases each break a correct trading rule:

  CORRECT RULE                 BIAS THAT ATTACKS IT      RESULT
  -----------------------      ---------------------     ------------------
  Cut losses short         <-- Loss aversion         --> Hold losers, hope
  Let winners run          <-- Loss aversion         --> Snatch tiny profits
  Trade only your setup    <-- Dopamine (action hit) --> Over-trade junk
  Trust tested edge        <-- Recency bias          --> Quit after 3 losses
  Each trade stands alone  <-- Sunk-cost fallacy     --> Chase the day's loss
```
*Figure 1 — Your instincts are not neutral; each one specifically attacks a rule you need.*

## Concept 2 — The Specific Account-Killers and the Exact Thought Behind Each

Biases are the raw material. In the live market they combine into named, recognisable behaviours. If you can catch the *exact sentence* running through your head, you can catch the behaviour before it costs you.

### Revenge trading

**The thought:** *"The market took my money — I'm going to take it back, right now."*

You take a loss (loss aversion delivers the sting). Instead of accepting it as a normal cost of business, you personalise it: the market "did this to you." Adrenaline and wounded ego push you to enter another trade immediately, usually bigger, usually without a valid setup, purely to erase the pain. This is the single most destructive pattern in trading. One ordinary ₹1,000 loss becomes a ₹1,000, then ₹2,500, then ₹6,000 spiral within twenty minutes.

### Over-trading

**The thought:** *"Something's happening, I don't want to miss it — let me just take this one."*

Driven by the dopamine action-hit and by boredom. Your plan may generate two or three valid signals a day, but you place fifteen trades because sitting still feels like doing nothing. Each marginal trade has no edge, and each one pays brokerage and STT (Securities Transaction Tax) and slippage. Over-trading bleeds an account by a thousand small cuts and, worse, exhausts the attention you need for the two good trades.

### FOMO — fear of missing out

**The thought:** *"It's already up 80 points, it's flying, if I don't get in NOW I'll miss the whole move."*

You watch Bank Nifty rip higher without you. The fear of watching a big move go by is so acute that you chase — buying at the top of an extended candle, far from any level, with a stop that is now huge or nonexistent. FOMO makes you enter at the worst possible price with the worst possible risk. The irony: the missed trade cost you nothing; the chased trade costs you real money.

### Moving or removing stops

**The thought:** *"It'll bounce back, I just need to give it a little more room."*

Price approaches your stop-loss. Loss aversion screams. So you drag the stop further away, or cancel it entirely, telling yourself you'll "watch it manually." You have now converted a defined, survivable risk into an unlimited one. This is how single trades wipe out weeks of gains. The stop is the one promise you make to yourself before emotion arrives; moving it is breaking that promise at the exact moment you are least fit to renegotiate.

### Doubling down / averaging into a loser

**The thought:** *"It's even cheaper now — I'll add more and lower my average, so it only needs a small bounce to break even."*

Also called averaging down. A position goes against you, so you add to it at the worse price. Now your loss per point is larger *and* your break-even point requires the market to reverse. If it keeps going, the losses compound. In intraday trading, where you have hours not months for a thesis to play out, this is a fast route to a margin call. Adding to winners can be valid; adding to losers to feel better is gambling.

```
The Tilt Spiral — how one small loss becomes a blown day:

        SMALL PLANNED LOSS (-1R)  <-- totally normal, expected
                 |
                 v
        EMOTION: sting + wounded ego ("that wasn't fair")
                 |
                 v
        REVENGE ENTRY (bigger size, no valid setup)
                 |
                 v
        BIGGER LOSS (-3R)  ->  "I have to make it back"
                 |
                 v
        DOUBLE DOWN + MOVE STOP  ->  loss aversion + sunk cost
                 |
                 v
        BLOWN DAILY LIMIT (-8R)  ->  shame, then either quit
                 |                    trading OR do it again tomorrow
                 v
        [CIRCUIT-BREAKER GOES HERE]  <-- the only exit from the spiral
```
*Figure 2 — Tilt is a feedback loop. Willpower rarely stops it once it starts; a pre-set rule does.*

## Concept 3 — Circuit-Breakers: Rules That Stop You Before You Stop Yourself

"Tilt" is the poker term for the emotional state where you abandon your strategy and play recklessly. You cannot reliably think your way out of tilt, because tilt degrades the very thinking you would use. The solution is mechanical: decide the limits in advance and make them non-negotiable, like a fuse that trips before the wiring burns.

### Fixed daily loss limit

Decide, before the session, the maximum you are willing to lose in one day — expressed in **R**, your risk unit (see Week 8: 1R = the rupee amount you risk on one trade, sized to 1–2% of capital). A common limit is **3R per day**. Hit minus-3R and you are done — flatten everything, close the platform, walk away. No "one more to get it back." The daily loss limit is the master switch that severs the tilt spiral at Figure 2's fourth step. Because you are paper-trading, honour it exactly as if it were real money; the habit is the whole point.

### Maximum trades per day

Cap the number of trades — for a beginner, **3 to 5 is plenty**. This directly kills over-trading and FOMO. If your best two setups have not appeared, taking a fifth mediocre trade to "use up" your count is not the idea; the cap is a ceiling, not a target. Knowing you have only three bullets makes you wait for the clean shot.

### Walk-away rules

Predefine the triggers that make you physically leave the screen for the day, regardless of P&L:

- **Two losses in a row** — step away for at least 30 minutes; you may be misreading conditions.
- **You broke a rule** (moved a stop, took an unplanned trade) — stop for the day even if you're up. Rule-breaking, not the loss, is the disease.
- **You feel it in your body** — racing heart, clenched jaw, the urge to "get it back." That physical state *is* tilt; walking away is the prescription.

### Checklists

A pre-trade checklist forces the slow, rational part of your brain to sign off before the fast, emotional part clicks buy. It is the same principle surgeons and pilots use: not because they are forgetful, but because checklists catch the errors that confidence hides. Your checklist converts "this feels right" into "this meets my written criteria — yes or no."

```
Pre-Trade Decision Checklist (must be all YES to enter):

  START
    |
    v
  [1] Is it within my session hours?    --NO--> DO NOT TRADE
    | YES
    v
  [2] Is this one of my written setups? --NO--> DO NOT TRADE
    | YES
    v
  [3] Is there a clear stop-loss level?  --NO--> DO NOT TRADE
    | YES
    v
  [4] Is reward:risk at least 1:2?       --NO--> DO NOT TRADE
    | YES
    v
  [5] Am I under my daily loss limit?    --NO--> STOP FOR THE DAY
    | YES
    v
  [6] Am I under my max trades?          --NO--> STOP FOR THE DAY
    | YES
    v
  [7] Am I calm (not chasing/angry)?     --NO--> WALK AWAY 30 MIN
    | YES
    v
  ==> ENTER TRADE, set stop-loss FIRST, then target
```
*Figure 3 — Any single NO halts the trade. The checklist is the gate emotion has to pass through.*

## Concept 4 — How a Written Trading Plan Removes In-the-Moment Decisions

Here is the core insight of the whole week: **the goal is to make zero important decisions while the market is open.** Every decision that matters — what to trade, when, how much, where the stop goes, when to quit — is made once, in writing, in a calm room, and then merely *executed* live.

Why does writing it down change anything? Because a decision made in advance is made by your rational, unhurried self. A decision made live is made by a brain flooded with dopamine and loss-aversion signals, under time pressure, with money on the line. These are effectively two different people, and the calm one is far smarter. The written plan lets the calm you overrule the panicked you — it is a contract you sign with yourself when you are at your best, to be obeyed when you are at your worst.

A plan also makes you **accountable and improvable**. If your rules are vague ("buy when it looks strong"), you can never tell whether a loss was bad luck or a broken rule, so you can never improve. If your rules are precise, every trade is either "followed the plan" or "didn't," and your journal (Week 9) becomes a feedback loop instead of a diary of regrets. Discipline is not a personality trait you either have or lack — it is the simple act of following a plan you already wrote.

## Concept 5 — Process Over Outcome: The Right Scoreboard

Beginners keep score with the day's profit. This is the wrong scoreboard, and it quietly trains the wrong habits. Because trading is probabilistic, a *good decision can lose* (a valid setup that the market happened to reject) and a *bad decision can win* (a reckless chase that happened to run). If you reward yourself for green days regardless of how you got there, you reinforce whatever you did on the winning days — including the rule-breaking.

The professional's scoreboard is **process quality**, measured trade by trade as "followed the plan" or "broke the plan." Over a large enough sample, following a positive-expectancy plan produces profit; that is arithmetic. Your job is not to win each trade — that is not in your control — but to execute your edge cleanly every time, which *is* in your control. Grade the swing, not the ball. When you make process the scoreboard, the tilt spiral loses its fuel, because a losing trade that followed the plan is no longer a failure to avenge — it is simply a cost of doing business you already priced in.

## Worked Examples

*All numbers below are hypothetical and illustrative — chosen to demonstrate mechanics, not to predict any real price. Assume this trader's capital gives 1R = ₹1,000, and their plan allows max 3 trades/day and a daily loss limit of −3R.*

### Example 1 — The tilt spiral, and where the circuit-breaker would have saved the day

**Instrument:** Bank Nifty (index; traded via lots in derivatives). **Date:** hypothetical Tuesday.

- **9:35 AM** — Trader takes a valid opening-range breakout long. Entry 48,200, stop 48,120 (risk = 80 points = 1R = ₹1,000), target 48,360 (1:2). Price stalls at 48,250, reverses, and hits the stop at 48,120. **Result: −1R (−₹1,000). Completely normal.**
- **9:48 AM** — The sting arrives. Thought: *"That was a fake-out, the market robbed me."* This is loss aversion plus personalisation — the entry point of Figure 2.
- **9:50 AM** — Without waiting for a setup, the trader re-enters long at 48,180 "to get it back," and to feel decisive, doubles size to 2R risk. No clean level, no checklist. Price drops to 48,020. **Result: −2R (−₹2,000). Running total: −3R.**
- **9:52 AM** — The daily loss limit (−3R) is now hit. The *plan* says: flatten, close platform, done for the day.

**Two endings.** *Without the circuit-breaker:* the trader thinks "I'm down ₹3,000, I can't end red," adds a third trade at 3R size (sunk-cost fallacy), and when it too fails, ends the day at roughly −7R (−₹7,000) — seven times a normal loss, all from one ordinary −1R start. *With the circuit-breaker:* the trader closes the platform at 9:52 AM at −3R, journals "revenge entry after first loss — broke setup rule," and walks. The −3R day is forgettable; it is fully recovered by two normal winning trades later in the week. The lesson is not that the first loss was bad — it was fine — but that *the reaction to it* is where accounts die.

### Example 2 — FOMO versus the checklist on a trending stock

**Instrument:** Reliance (cash, intraday under MIS). **Date:** hypothetical Thursday.

- **10:15 AM** — Reliance has run from 2,900 to 2,948 in twenty minutes on a news pop. It is all over social media. The trader feels the pull: *"It's flying, I'll miss it, buy now."* Classic FOMO — wanting to enter at the top of an extended move.
- **The checklist intervenes (Figure 3):** Q2 — Is this my written setup? The trader's setup is "pullback to a rising level," not "chase a vertical candle." **NO.** Trade rejected.
- **10:16 AM** — Instead of chasing, the trader marks 2,930 as the breakout level the stock just cleared, and waits.
- **10:52 AM** — Reliance pulls back to 2,933, near the reclaimed level, and prints a bullish reversal candle. Now the checklist runs clean: within hours (yes), written setup (yes — pullback to level), clear stop at 2,922 below the level (yes, risk = 11 points), target 2,955 (yes, reward 22 points = 1:2), under limits (yes), calm (yes, because the trader is executing a plan, not chasing). **Entry 2,933, stop 2,922 (1R = ₹1,000 at this trader's share quantity), target 2,955.**
- **11:40 AM** — Target hit at 2,955. **Result: +2R (+₹2,000).**

**The point:** the FOMO entry at 2,948 would have had its stop far away and a poor reward:risk, and would likely have been shaken out on the very pullback the disciplined trader used as an entry. Same stock, same move — the checklist turned a −1R chase into a +2R planned trade. Waiting is a position.

## Common Mistakes (and the fix)

### 1. "I'll just be more disciplined tomorrow."

**Why beginners do it:** they treat discipline as willpower — a feeling they can summon. When they blow up, they promise to try harder. **The fix:** discipline is not willpower, it is *infrastructure*. You do not "try" not to over-trade; you set a max-trades cap and a loss limit that mechanically stop you. Build the fuse; don't rely on feeling brave near the fire.

### 2. Treating the daily loss limit as a suggestion.

**Why beginners do it:** the limit feels arbitrary in the moment — "I'm only down 3R, one more trade could turn it around." Recency bias and sunk cost whisper that the next trade is different. **The fix:** the limit is a hard stop, not a mood. Physically close the trading platform when you hit it. The habit of obeying it in paper trading is exactly what will save your capital in Week 12.

### 3. Journaling only the P&L, not the psychology.

**Why beginners do it:** the number feels like the whole story. **The fix:** in your journal, tag every trade "followed plan" or "broke plan," and note the *thought* you had at entry ("felt I'd miss it," "wanted revenge"). Over a month you will see that most losses cluster in the "broke plan" bucket — which is fantastic news, because broken rules are fixable in a way that market randomness is not.

### 4. Confusing a good outcome with a good decision.

**Why beginners do it:** if a rule-breaking chase happens to win, the brain files it as "that worked." **The fix:** grade the *decision*, not the result. A rule-breaking trade that wins is still a bad trade — you were paid to reinforce a habit that will eventually ruin you. Praise process-followed losses; distrust process-broken wins.

### 5. Setting a plan you can't actually follow.

**Why beginners do it:** they write "I will take zero losses" or "trade only perfect A+ setups" — heroic but impossible, so they abandon the plan on day one. **The fix:** write realistic rules (losses are expected; three trades is fine). A plan you can keep 95% of the time beats a perfect plan you keep 0% of the time.

## Nuances & Edge Cases

- **The best days often have the fewest trades.** Beginners equate activity with progress. Some of the most profitable sessions are one clean trade and then patience. Boredom is not a signal to act.
- **Winning streaks are more dangerous than losing streaks.** Losses make you cautious; wins make you reckless. After three green days, recency bias tells you you've "figured it out" and you size up right before a normal losing trade — now magnified. Keep size constant regardless of the last few results.
- **Tilt has a physical signature.** Learn yours: shallow breathing, leaning toward the screen, the specific urge to "get it back." These body cues arrive *before* the bad click. Treat them as a stop-loss for your mind.
- **The ~3:20 PM auto square-off is a discipline trap.** Under MIS (Margin Intraday Square-off — the intraday product with leverage), the broker auto-closes open positions around 3:20 PM. Beginners, seeing they are red near the close, panic-trade in the last ten minutes to "fix" the day before the system flattens them. Late-session desperation trades are almost pure tilt; your plan should simply bar new entries after a set time (say 3:00 PM). CNC (Cash and Carry, the delivery product) is not auto-squared-off, but it is not for intraday and does not save a losing intraday idea — refusing to convert a failing MIS trade to CNC "to avoid the loss" is itself a discipline test.
- **A plan is a living document, but only edited when the market is closed.** You may revise rules on the weekend based on your journal. You may *never* revise them at 10:30 AM to justify a trade you want to take. Edits happen in the calm room, never in the arena.
- **Paper trading has a hidden psychology gap.** Fake money reduces the fear, so tilt is milder now than it will be with real capital. Do not conclude "I'm disciplined." Assume the emotions intensify sharply in Week 12, and over-build your habits now so they survive the upgrade.

## Day-by-Day (Mon–Fri)

- **Monday — Name your enemy.** Read Concepts 1 and 2. Then open your Week 9 trade journal and label your worst three trades with the exact killer behind each (revenge / over-trade / FOMO / moved stop / doubled down). *Observation target:* find the recurring thought that precedes your worst trades — most people have one signature pattern.
- **Tuesday — Watch tilt live in yourself.** Paper-trade one setup only. If you take a loss, do not re-enter for 15 minutes; instead, write down the sentence going through your head and the physical sensation. *Observation target:* catch the body signature of the urge to "get it back."
- **Wednesday — Build your circuit-breakers.** Decide your numbers: risk per trade (1R in ₹), daily loss limit (e.g. −3R), max trades (e.g. 3), session hours, and a no-new-entry cutoff time. Write them down. *Observation target:* pick numbers you can actually obey, not heroic ones.
- **Thursday — Draft your one-page plan** using the template below. Print it or keep it open on-screen while you paper-trade. Run every entry through the Figure 3 checklist out loud. *Observation target:* notice how many trades the checklist rejects — that rejection is the plan working.
- **Friday — Trade the plan, then grade the plan.** Paper-trade the full session strictly by the plan. In the evening, grade each trade "followed / broke" — not by profit. *Observation target:* your "followed-plan %" for the day; aim to make that number, not the P&L, your scoreboard.

## Key Takeaways

- Your brain is wired against you: loss aversion, dopamine, recency bias, and sunk cost each specifically attack a rule you need to follow. You cannot out-feel them — you must out-rule them.
- The account-killers are named and recognisable — revenge trading, over-trading, FOMO, moving stops, doubling down — and each has an exact thought you can learn to catch mid-sentence.
- Tilt is a feedback loop, not a mood; only a pre-set circuit-breaker (daily loss limit, max trades, walk-away rules, checklist) reliably severs it.
- A written plan works because it lets your calm, rational self make the important decisions in advance, so your panicked live self only has to execute.
- Grade decisions, not outcomes: a rule-broken win is still a bad trade; a rule-followed loss is a good trade. Your scoreboard is "followed-plan %," not daily P&L.

## This Week's Milestone

You have a completed, signed **one-page trading plan** (Figure 4) with concrete numbers for risk per trade, daily loss limit, max trades, session hours, and a written setup list — and you have paper-traded at least one full session strictly by it, then graded every trade "followed" versus "broke" the plan rather than by profit. The proof the week is done is not a green day; it is a day where you can honestly write "100% followed the plan," win or lose.
